# How AI TL;DR Actually Works in Hutch (And Why It's Not Slop)

The word "slop" exists because most AI-generated content is garbage. I know it. You know it. The term caught on because it accurately describes the flood of hollow, machine-written text polluting every corner of the internet: SEO articles that say nothing in 2,000 words, LinkedIn posts that feel like they were extruded from a tube, "key takeaways" listicles that flatten nuance into bullet points nobody asked for.

So when a read-it-later app announces "AI summaries," your scepticism is warranted. Here's why Hutch's TL;DR is different, and how it works under the hood.

## You choose what to read. The AI doesn't.

The most important distinction isn't technical. It's philosophical.

Most AI-driven content features try to curate _for_ you. They decide what's interesting, what's relevant, what you should see next. That's the algorithmic feed model, and it's the reason people save articles to read-it-later apps in the first place: to escape the machine-curated timeline and read on their own terms.

Hutch's TL;DR doesn't curate anything. You saved the article. You chose it. The summary exists for one reason: to help you decide _when_ to read it.

You're looking at your queue on a Tuesday morning with 15 minutes before a meeting. You've got 30 saved articles. The TL;DR tells you which ones are quick hits and which ones need an hour of focused attention. It's a triage tool, not a replacement for reading.

## What the summary is (and isn't)

Each TL;DR is a short plain-text summary of the article's core argument or finding. No markdown formatting. No "5 Key Takeaways" headers. No extracted quotes. Just two or three sentences that tell you what the piece is actually about.

The prompt that generates these summaries has a banned-words list that reads like a greatest hits of AI slop: "leverage," "paradigm shift," "game-changer," "holistic approach," "seamless integration." If the summary sounds like a press release, the prompt is broken and I'll fix it.

The rules are strict: active voice, short sentences, plain connectors, specific facts where available. No semicolons, no em dashes, no corporate jargon. Write like a person explaining an article to a friend.

## The technical implementation

Summaries are generated by DeepSeek V3 (the `deepseek-chat` model). I chose DeepSeek for this job because it's good at concise factual summarisation and the economics work at scale. The cost is absorbed into the subscription. You don't pay per summary.

Here's the part that matters most: **one summary per URL, cached globally**.

When you save an article, Hutch checks a cache first. If someone else has already saved the same URL, you get the same summary. No API call, no cost, no latency. If it's a cache miss, DeepSeek generates the summary and it gets stored for everyone.

This design has two benefits:

**Cost stays manageable.** A per-user model where every person gets a personalised summary of the same article would burn through API credits. Global caching means popular articles cost one API call total, regardless of how many people save them.

**No personalisation bias.** Every user sees the same summary for the same article. There's no filter bubble, no subtle reframing based on your reading history or preferences. The summary describes what the article says. That's it.

There's a minimum content threshold too. Articles shorter than about 800 visible characters don't get summarised. If it's already short enough to scan, a summary adds nothing.

## Why not just use Claude or GPT-4?

I tested multiple models. For this specific task (short, factual, no-nonsense summarisation of web articles), DeepSeek V3 hits the sweet spot of quality and cost. The summaries are capped at 750 characters. You don't need a frontier model to write three accurate sentences.

Could I use Claude? Yes, and it's used as a fallback in some code paths. But for the default summarisation pipeline that runs at save time, DeepSeek does the job well and keeps the per-article cost low enough that I can offer it without metering.

## The philosophy: read the web, not the slop

Hutch exists because I believe reading still matters. Not skimming, not "consuming content," not letting an algorithm drip-feed you engagement-optimised fragments. Actual reading.

The TL;DR feature might seem contradictory to that. It's not. It's the same reason a librarian puts shelf cards on books: so you can find the one you want to sit down with.

The summary helps you choose what to read. Then you read it.

That's the line I won't cross. Hutch will never generate "smart highlights" that let you skip the article. It won't produce AI-written commentary on what you saved. It won't synthesise your reading list into a daily briefing. Those features would make Hutch a tool for avoiding reading, and that's the opposite of what it's for.

Read the web, not the slop.
