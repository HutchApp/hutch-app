# A System for Technical Reading That Actually Works

You have a bookmark folder called "To Read." You last opened it in 2023. It holds somewhere between 40 and 80 articles. You stopped counting.

You saved them with real intent. A deep dive on database indexing. A post on microservice tradeoffs. That essay on distributed systems everyone shared last year.

You read maybe five of them.

The other 45 sit there, decaying into irrelevance. Some links are dead. Some advice is outdated. The guilt of not reading them has turned "saving an article" into self-deception. You're not saving it to read later. You're saving it to feel like you will.

I lived this for years. Then I built a system that fixed it.

## Why your reading list is a graveyard

It's not a willpower problem. It's a systems problem. Three pieces are missing.

**No triage.** Every article goes into the same pile with equal weight. A 20-minute piece on consensus algorithms sits next to a 2-minute CSS grid tip. When everything has the same priority, nothing gets read.

**No ritual.** You save articles in moments of curiosity. You never set aside time to read them. Reading needs a different kind of attention than scrolling. Without a dedicated slot, scrolling wins every time.

**No friction reduction.** When you do open an article, you face cookie banners, newsletter popups, sidebar ads, and autoplaying videos. The reading experience is hostile. Of course you close the tab.

Fix those three things and the whole system works. I know because I've run this system for over 10 years, refining it until it became second nature.

## The system

### Save everything

This is counterintuitive, but it's the most important step. When you find an interesting article, save it right away. Don't evaluate whether it's "worth reading." Don't categorize it. Don't open it in a new tab "to read in a minute."

Save it. Move on.

Why does filtering at save time fail? It feels productive, but it's procrastination dressed as curation. You spend mental energy judging an article before you've read it. That's backwards. Evaluation comes later, during triage.

Your save gesture needs to be frictionless. One click, one keyboard shortcut, one tap. If saving takes more than two seconds, you'll make excuses.

### Triage weekly with AI summaries

Once a week, open your reading list and scan it. I do mine on Sunday mornings. The goal is not to read. The goal is to sort.

AI summaries changed everything for me. Instead of opening each article to figure out whether it's worth 15 minutes, I read a two-sentence summary. That's usually enough to decide.

The triage is simple. Star the ones worth reading. Archive the rest. No guilt. An archived article isn't a failure. It's a decision. You looked at it, decided it wasn't worth your time right now, and moved on. That beats letting it rot in a list you'll never reopen.

I triage 15 to 20 articles in under 10 minutes. Out of those, 5 to 7 get starred. That's my reading for the week.

### Read in reader view

This sounds minor. It isn't. Never read articles on the original site if you can avoid it. Use a reader view that strips away the noise: no ads, no popups, no sidebar widgets, no "related articles" pulling your attention sideways.

Dark mode if you prefer it. A readable font at a comfortable size. Just the text and the images the author included.

The difference is large. Articles that felt like a chore on the original site become enjoyable once the hostility is gone. I started finishing pieces I'd bounced off before. Not because I had more discipline. Because the experience stopped fighting me.

### Build a rhythm

You need recurring, predictable slots for reading. "I'll read when I have time" doesn't work. You'll never have time. You have to make it.

Here's what works for me:

- **Morning coffee** = 1 article. Something short, 5 to 10 minutes. A good way to start the day with something that isn't email or social media.
- **Commute** = 1 article. If you take transit, this is free reading time. If you drive, pick a different slot.
- **Weekend morning** = deep reads. Saturday or Sunday, I set aside 30 to 45 minutes for the longer, denser pieces. The ones that need real concentration.

The specific slots don't matter. Consistency does. After a few weeks, reaching for an article during morning coffee becomes as automatic as reaching for the coffee.

## The compound effect

The system compounds. When you read the things you save, you start saving better things. Your sense for what's worth your attention sharpens. The ratio of starred to archived during triage improves week over week.

After 10 years, my reading has shaped how I think about software, architecture, and building products. Not because I read more than anyone else, but because I built a system that made sure the reading happened.
