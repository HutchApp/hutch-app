# A System for Technical Reading That Actually Works

You have a bookmark folder called "To Read." You last opened it in 2023. There are somewhere between 40 and 80 articles in there — you're not sure because you stopped counting. You saved them with genuine intent. A deep dive into database indexing. A post about the tradeoffs of microservices. That one essay on distributed systems everyone was sharing.

You read maybe five of them.

The other 45 are still there, slowly decaying into irrelevance. Some links are dead. Some advice is outdated. And the guilt of not reading them has quietly turned "saving an article" into an act of self-deception — you're not saving it to read later, you're saving it to feel like you will.

I know this because I lived it for years. And then I built a system that actually fixed it.

## Why your reading list is a graveyard

It's not a willpower problem. It's a systems problem. Specifically, three missing pieces:

**No triage.** Every article goes into the same pile with equal priority. The 20-minute deep dive on consensus algorithms sits next to a 2-minute tip about CSS grid. When everything is important, nothing is.

**No ritual.** You save articles in moments of curiosity, but you never carve out time to actually read them. Reading requires a different mode of attention than scrolling, and without a dedicated slot, scrolling always wins.

**No friction reduction.** When you finally do open an article, you're hit with cookie banners, newsletter popups, sidebar ads, and autoplaying videos. The reading experience is actively hostile. Of course you close the tab.

Fix these three things and the whole system starts working. I know because I've been running this system for over 10 years, refining it gradually until it became second nature.

## The system

### 1. Save everything. Don't filter at save time.

This is counterintuitive, but it's the most important part. When you find an interesting article, save it immediately. Don't evaluate whether it's "worth reading." Don't try to categorise it. Don't open it in a new tab "to read in a minute."

Just save it and move on.

The reason: filtering at save time feels productive, but it's actually procrastination dressed up as curation. You spend mental energy deciding whether something is worth your time *before you've even read it*. That's backwards. Evaluation happens later, during triage.

Your save gesture should be frictionless — one click, one keyboard shortcut, one tap. If saving takes more than two seconds, you'll start making excuses not to do it.

### 2. Triage weekly with AI summaries

Once a week — I do mine on Sunday mornings — open your reading list and scan it. Not to read, just to sort.

This is where AI summaries changed everything for me. Instead of opening each article to figure out whether it's worth 15 minutes of my life, I read a two-sentence summary. Most of the time, that's enough to decide.

The triage is simple: star the ones worth reading. Archive the rest. No guilt. An archived article isn't a failure — it's a decision. You looked at it, decided it wasn't worth your time right now, and moved on. That's better than letting it rot in a list you'll never revisit.

I triage about 15–20 articles in under 10 minutes. Out of those, maybe 5–7 get starred. That's my reading for the week.

### 3. Read in reader view

This one sounds minor but it's transformative. Never read articles on the original site if you can avoid it. Use a reader view that strips everything away: no ads, no popups, no sidebar widgets, no related articles pulling your attention sideways.

Dark mode if you prefer it. A readable font at a comfortable size. Just the text and whatever images the author included.

The difference is dramatic. Articles that felt like a chore on the original site become genuinely enjoyable when the hostility is gone. I started finishing pieces I'd previously bounced off of — not because I had more discipline, but because the experience stopped fighting me.

### 4. Build a rhythm

This is where the system becomes self-sustaining. You need recurring, predictable slots for reading. Not "I'll read when I have time" — you'll never have time. You have to make time.

Here's what works for me:

- **Morning coffee** = 1 article. Something short, 5–10 minutes. A good way to start the day with something that isn't email or social media.
- **Commute** = 1 article. If you take transit, this is free reading time. If you drive, save it for a different slot.
- **Weekend morning** = deep reads. Saturday or Sunday, I set aside 30–45 minutes for the longer, denser pieces. The ones that require actual concentration.

The specific slots don't matter. What matters is that they're consistent. After a few weeks, reaching for an article during morning coffee becomes as automatic as reaching for the coffee itself.

## The compound effect

Here's what surprised me most: the system compounds. When you actually read the things you save, you start saving better things. Your instinct for what's worth your attention sharpens. The ratio of starred-to-archived during triage improves.

After 10 years, my reading has shaped how I think about software, architecture, and building products. Not because I read more than anyone else, but because I built a system that made sure the reading actually happened.

## Why I built Hutch

Eventually I got tired of duct-taping this system together with browser bookmarks, Pocket (which shut down), Omnivore (which also shut down), and various note-taking apps. So I built the tool I actually wanted.

[Hutch](https://www.hutch.page) is a read-it-later app designed around this exact workflow: fast saving, AI-powered triage, a clean reader view, and nothing else getting in the way. I built it for myself, and I use it every day.

But the system works regardless of what tool you use. The tool just removes friction. The system is what changes your reading life.

Start with the rhythm. The rest follows.
