# Hutch vs Instapaper: Two Different Approaches to Read-It-Later

*Published April 2026*

When Pocket shut down in July 2025, a lot of people went looking for a new home for their reading list. Some moved to Omnivore — until that shut down too. Others landed on Instapaper, which has been around since 2008 and still works exactly the way you'd expect a read-it-later app to work.

Hutch launched around the same time, built from a different starting point entirely. I'd been running my own reading system for about ten years before turning it into a product.

Both apps save articles for later. Beyond that, they take genuinely different approaches. Here's an honest look at how they compare.

## The basics

Instapaper and Hutch do the same core thing: you find something on the web, save it, and read it when you're ready. Both strip away the clutter and give you a clean reading experience. Both let you organise what you've saved.

That's where the overlap mostly ends.

## Instapaper: the safe, familiar choice

Instapaper is the closest thing to Pocket that still exists. If you used Pocket for years and want something that works the same way, Instapaper is the natural landing spot.

It's mature, stable, and has native mobile apps for iOS and Android. The reading experience is clean and distraction-free. It's the default read-it-later app on Kobo e-readers, which is a nice integration if you read on one. The interface is simple and stays out of your way.

Instapaper offers a free tier that covers the basics — saving articles, reading them later, basic organisation. A premium tier adds full-text search, speed reading, and text-to-speech.

It's US-based, owned by Instant Paper, Inc., and has been through a few ownership changes over the years (Betaworks, Pinterest, and then back to independent).

The honest trade-off: Instapaper hasn't seen significant feature development recently. It works, and it works well, but it's largely the same product it was a few years ago. For some people, that's a feature — stability is underrated. For others, it feels like a product in maintenance mode.

## Hutch: a different bet

Hutch comes at the problem differently. It's a newer product, built by a solo developer in Australia, and it's opinionated about where read-it-later should go next.

The biggest difference is AI. Hutch uses AI to generate summaries of saved articles, helping you triage your reading list and decide what's worth your time before committing to a full read. Instapaper has no AI features at all.

Whether that matters to you depends on how you read. If you save three articles a week and read all of them, AI summaries might not change much for you. If you save thirty articles a week and struggle to get through them, having a quick summary to help you prioritise can be the difference between a useful reading list and an ever-growing pile of guilt.

Hutch is browser-first — it's a web app with a browser extension, not a native mobile app. That means it works on any device with a browser, but it doesn't have the deep OS integration that a native app provides. There's no share sheet on your phone, no offline reading on the train (yet). If mobile apps are essential to your workflow, that's a real gap right now.

Hutch is hosted in Australia, which matters if you care about data jurisdiction. Your reading data stays on Australian infrastructure under Australian privacy law.

On pricing: Hutch offered its first 100 members a free founding-member tier. After that, it's A$3.99 per month. There's no free tier in the way Instapaper offers one — Hutch is a paid product from the start.

The trade-off on the other side: Hutch is actively shipping features. It's a product that's visibly evolving, with regular updates and a clear development roadmap. That momentum is real, but so is the risk that comes with any newer product.

## Who should pick what

**Pick Instapaper if:**

- You want a direct Pocket replacement that works the way Pocket did
- Native mobile apps are non-negotiable for your reading workflow
- You read on a Kobo e-reader and want built-in integration
- You prefer a free tier to get started
- You value a mature, stable product over new features

**Pick Hutch if:**

- You save more than you can read and want AI to help you triage
- You're comfortable with a browser-based workflow
- Data sovereignty matters to you (Australian hosting)
- You want a product that's actively evolving with new features
- You're willing to pay for a tool built by someone who uses it every day

## The honest take

Instapaper is the closest thing to Pocket that still exists. If you want exactly what Pocket was — a clean, simple, reliable read-it-later app with mobile apps and a free tier — it's the safe choice. It does that job well and has for nearly two decades.

If you want AI summaries and a product that's actively evolving, Hutch is a different bet. It's newer, it's smaller, and it doesn't have native mobile apps. But it's built with a clear vision for where reading tools should go, and it's shipping toward that vision every week.

Neither choice is wrong. They're just solving the same problem from different directions.
